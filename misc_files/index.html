The Women In Male Dominated Streets

Data from the NYC Department of Records shows the gender disparity in our urban landscape

By Anusha Subramanian • Published April 11th, 2025

For centuries, the naming of streets has been more than a matter of navigation—it's been a reflection of who and what we value as a society. In New York City, one of the most diverse and progressive cities in the world, there exists a stark disparity in representation: of the thousands of streets named after notable individuals, only a small fraction honor women.


This investigation explores this gender gap in NYC's street names, uncovering patterns of underrepresentation and highlighting the remarkable women who have managed to secure their place in the city's geographical nomenclature. Through data visualization and storytelling, we'll examine not just the numbers, but also the stories behind the names—and what they reveal about our collective memory and values.

Breaking Down NYC's Street Name Map

Exploring the gender distribution across New York City's street naming landscape reveals distinct patterns of representation.

The Majority: Streets Named After Men

Streets named after men dominate New York City's landscape, accounting for 88% of all honorific street names. This overwhelming majority reflects centuries of naming practices that have prioritized male historical figures, politicians, war heroes, and community leaders.

These male-named streets are distributed across all five boroughs, with particular concentration in Manhattan's grid system and the oldest parts of Brooklyn and the Bronx. Many date back to the city's early development, with waves of naming corresponding to major historical events like the Revolutionary War, Civil War, and World Wars.

The Minority: Streets Named After Women

Streets named after women represent just 12% of NYC's honorific street names—a stark underrepresentation considering women make up 51% of the city's population. These streets are not evenly distributed, with notable concentrations in newer developments and areas that have undergone recent renaming initiatives.

The timeline of women's representation on street signs tells an important story: while men's names date back to the city's founding, the first woman-named street didn't appear until the late 19th century. Even more telling, over 70% of streets named after women were designated after 1990, reflecting changing awareness of gender representation issues.

Eleanor Roosevelt

1884-1962

When Eleanor Roosevelt Boulevard was dedicated in Queens in 1958, it was one of the rare instances where a woman was honored while still living—a testament to her extraordinary influence and respect. As First Lady, diplomat, activist, and author, Roosevelt redefined the role of women in American politics.

Born to privilege but acquainted with personal tragedy, Roosevelt transformed herself from a shy debutante into a fearless advocate for civil rights, women's rights, and the rights of the poor and disadvantaged. Her weekly newspaper column "My Day" ran for 27 years, giving Americans unprecedented access to the thoughts of a First Lady.

After her husband's death, Roosevelt continued her public service as a U.S. delegate to the United Nations, where she chaired the committee that drafted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights—a document she considered her greatest achievement.

Eleanor Roosevelt Boulevard
Borough: Queens
Named in: 1958
Location: Connects Forest Hills with Kew Gardens
Fun fact: Roosevelt was present at the street naming ceremony, making her one of the few people to witness her own street naming in NYC.

Shirley Chisholm

1924-2005

"Unbought and unbossed" was the slogan of Shirley Chisholm's historic 1972 presidential campaign—the first campaign by a Black woman for a major party's nomination. But long before breaking that barrier, Chisholm was already making history as the first Black woman elected to Congress in 1968, representing her Brooklyn district.

Born in Brooklyn to Caribbean immigrant parents, Chisholm's roots in the community ran deep. A former teacher and childcare center director, she brought her passion for education and children's welfare to Albany as a state assemblywoman before heading to Washington.

In Congress, she was known for her outspoken advocacy for women, minorities, and the poor. She helped found both the National Women's Political Caucus and the Congressional Black Caucus, organizations that continue her legacy of advocacy today.

Shirley Chisholm Place
Borough: Brooklyn
Named in: 2009
Location: Intersection of Fulton Street and St. James Place in Bedford-Stuyvesant
Fun fact: The street is located near her former congressional district office.

Billie Holiday

1915-1959

Billie Holiday transformed American music through her distinctive vocal style and emotional intensity. Despite a life marked by hardship and discrimination, "Lady Day," as she was known, created a legacy that continues to influence artists across genres.

Born Eleanora Fagan in Philadelphia, Holiday spent much of her youth in Baltimore before moving to Harlem as a teenager. It was in the nightclubs of Harlem that she began singing, developing her unique improvisational technique that would revolutionize vocal jazz.

Beyond her musical innovations, Holiday showed tremendous courage in performing "Strange Fruit," a haunting protest against the lynching of Black Americans. The song, which she first performed in 1939, is considered one of the first explicit protest songs of the civil rights movement.

Billie Holiday Place
Borough: Manhattan
Named in: 1979
Location: West 141st Street between Seventh and Eighth Avenues in Harlem
Fun fact: The street is near the site of the former Alhambra Ballroom, where Holiday performed early in her career.

Beyond Binary: Groups & Organizations

Not all street names honor individuals. Throughout NYC, streets commemorate groups, organizations, concepts, and geographical features. These names represent an important aspect of the city's naming practices, often reflecting community values and landmarks.

Notable examples include streets named after religious institutions, civic organizations, indigenous tribes that once inhabited the area, and natural features. These names tend to be concentrated in areas of historical significance or near the institutions they reference.

The disparity in street naming is not merely a statistical curiosity—it reflects and reinforces broader patterns of historical erasure and underrepresentation.
